The question

Is the Earth getting greener or browner?

Using NASA MODIS NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) satellite data spanning 2001-2024, this visualization maps the trend in vegetation cover across the globe. Each dot represents a 3°×3° grid cell; its color shows whether vegetation has increased (green), decreased (brown), or stayed stable (tan) over two decades.

Click any dot to see its full 24-year time series. Use the region selector to zoom into areas of interest.

Design rationale

Why this visualization works

Discrete color bins: five distinct colors (brown through green) make it easy to classify each dot at a glance — no need to estimate a continuous gradient. Thresholds align with meaningful cutoffs for vegetation change.

Dot grid over choropleth: each 3° grid cell is its own mark, avoiding the area-bias of choropleth maps (large regions dominate visually). This gives equal visual weight to all locations.

Details-on-demand: clicking a dot reveals its full 24-year time series with a regression trend line, and Shift+drag lets you lasso any region to see summary statistics for all dots inside.

Region presets: pre-selected regions guide users to the most interesting stories (India's greening, Amazon's browning) while still allowing free exploration.

Development process

How we built it

Data pipeline: we fetched MODIS NDVI imagery from NASA GIBS for July of each year 2001–2024, extracted numerical NDVI values from the color-mapped PNGs, and computed per-pixel linear regression slopes.

D3.js implementation: the map uses an equirectangular projection with SVG circle marks. Interactions use D3's brush, zoom, and event handling.
