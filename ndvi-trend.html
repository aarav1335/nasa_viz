The question

Is the Earth getting greener or browner?

Using NASA MODIS NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) satellite data spanning 2001-2024, this visualization maps the linear trend in vegetation cover across the globe. For each 2°×2° grid cell, we computed the per-year linear regression slope from July-only NDVI measurements. Each dot's color shows whether vegetation has increased (green), decreased (brown), or stayed stable (tan) over two decades.

Click any dot to see its full 24-year time series. Shift+drag to select a region and view summary statistics. Use the region selector to zoom into areas of interest.

Design rationale

Why this visualization works

Discrete color bins over continuous gradient: we tested a continuous green→brown ramp first, but found it made subtle trends hard to read. Five discrete bins (brown → tan → green) let viewers classify each dot at a glance. Thresholds at ±0.001 and ±0.003 NDVI/year align with ecologically meaningful vegetation change rates.

Dot grid over choropleth: a choropleth would bias perception toward large landmasses (e.g., Siberia, Sahara) purely by area. The equispaced 2°×2° dot grid gives equal visual weight to every location, making regional hot-spots easier to spot.

Details-on-demand + brushing: clicking a dot reveals its real 24-year annual NDVI time series with a regression trend line. Shift+drag draws a rectangular selection to show aggregate stats (mean trend, greening/browning counts) for any group of cells — enabling both micro and macro exploration.

Region presets: pre-selected regions (Amazon, India, Sahel, etc.) guide users to the most interesting stories while still allowing free exploration via the dropdown. Each preset is motivated by real-world environmental narratives (deforestation, agricultural intensification, climate-driven greening).

Development process

How we built it

Data pipeline: we fetched MODIS NDVI imagery from NASA GIBS for July of each year 2001–2024, extracted numerical NDVI values from the color-mapped PNGs, and computed per-pixel linear regression slopes.

D3.js implementation: the map uses an equirectangular projection with SVG circle marks. Interactions use D3's brush, zoom, and event handling.
