The question

How do surface temperature and vegetation patterns vary across place and season?

This visualization uses NASA MODIS imagery to compare land surface temperature and Enhanced Vegetation Index (EVI) across selected world regions. By changing the region, year, and season, users can explore how surface heat and vegetation patterns shift across different environments.

Design rationale

Why this visualization works

This visualization compares land surface temperature and vegetation patterns across regions, years, and seasons. We chose a paired-map layout because the main goal is comparison: users can view temperature and Enhanced Vegetation Index (EVI) side by side for the same region and time period. This makes it easier to see whether hotter land surface conditions correspond with lower vegetation density, stronger seasonal contrast, or regional differences in surface cover.

The primary visual encoding is geographic color imagery from NASA MODIS layers. This choice works well because both temperature and vegetation are spatial phenomena, and map-based color patterns allow users to quickly identify broad environmental differences across a region. We considered using a scatterplot of temperature versus vegetation, but that would have removed the geographic context. Since the project question focuses on how patterns vary across regions and seasons, preserving spatial context was more important than showing only a statistical relationship.

The interaction design uses region, year, and season controls as dynamic filters. These controls let users focus on one geographic area and time period at a time instead of being overwhelmed by the full global dataset. The seasonal selector is especially important because land surface temperature and vegetation both change throughout the year. By switching between spring, summer, fall, and winter, users can explore temporal variation without needing to compare many separate static maps.

The two-map layout also supports a clear visual task: compare the same place across two environmental variables. Temperature is shown on the left and vegetation is shown on the right, so users can scan horizontally to identify whether the patterns align or diverge. This structure keeps the page focused and avoids making users switch between separate tabs or hidden layers to compare the two variables.

From a data transformation perspective, the visualization uses NASA GIBS imagery for selected MODIS layers and filters the map by bounding boxes for each region. Instead of downloading and plotting every raw pixel manually, the visualization requests map layers for the selected region, date, and variable. This keeps the interaction responsive while still allowing users to explore meaningful spatial and seasonal patterns in the MODIS dataset.

Development process

Our team split the work across data exploration, interface design, and D3 implementation. One part of the group explored the MODIS dataset and identified which variables would best support the project question. Another part focused on building the webpage layout, region controls, and visual styling. The D3 implementation involved connecting the dropdown controls to NASA GIBS requests, rendering the selected map layers, and updating the captions based on user input.

The most time-consuming parts were scoping the project and making the interactions reliable. We initially considered showing more variables and more regions, but narrowed the visualization to land surface temperature and EVI so the final page stayed focused. We also spent time testing different region bounding boxes and dates to make sure the selected views loaded consistently and showed interpretable patterns. Overall, the development process was iterative: we started with exploratory maps, chose the clearest comparison, and then refined the controls and page layout around that question.
